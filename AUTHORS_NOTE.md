# A Note from Kino

I won't tell you my gender. My religion is my own business. The colour of my skin is irrelevant. Where I live doesn't matter. Whether I love men, women, both, or neither has no bearing on a single line of this code.

I'm telling you this not because those things aren't real, but because they aren't *yours*. This project isn't about me. If you want your instance of FaceChan to be about those things — to celebrate them, to centre them, to build community around them — then do it. Fork it. Make it yours. That's exactly what it's for.

What I will tell you is this: I'm a parent. And I'm a programmer. Those two things, more than anything else, are why this software exists.

---

## How it started

A few years ago I got time-banned from Facebook for a post where I questioned something. I don't even remember what. That's how unremarkable it was. I sat there staring at the ban notice thinking: I built things on the internet before Mark Zuckerberg was old enough to drive. I don't need his permission to have an opinion.

I'd had a half-formed idea for years — write my own Facebook. Not to compete with Meta. Just to have somewhere that was mine. The ban pushed it from fantasy to something I actually started sketching out.

Then during one of those bans I found 4chan. I'm not especially proud of this. But I'd be lying if I said I wasn't immediately hooked on parts of it. Posting anonymously is a genuinely strange experience — stranger than I expected. You say things differently when nobody knows it's you. Sometimes worse things. Often more honest things. Usually both.

My boards are /ck (cooking), /g (tech), /out (outdoors), /an (animals) — and yes, /b. Everyone on /b knows it's cancer. That's not an excuse, it's just true. It doesn't stop the ylyl threads from occasionally producing something that makes you laugh until you can't breathe. /b is the worst of the internet and sometimes, just occasionally, something human crawls out of it. I find that strangely moving.

---

## The hybrid idea

The Facebook clone kept nagging at me. But I don't have big corporation money — and I have no interest in becoming a big corporation to get it. 4chan's model suddenly seemed like the answer. A fixed number of boards. A fixed number of threads. A fixed number of replies. When a thread fills up, it's gone. Cheap to run. Clean. Honest about impermanence in a way that Facebook, with its infinite scroll of memories, never is.

The UX problem was real though. 4chan is difficult for new users — the culture, the conventions, the way threads work. Facebook, for all its sins, has spent twenty years perfecting an interface that billions of people find intuitive. So why not use that as the template? Take what Meta got right about UX and graft it onto what 4chan got right about anonymity and impermanence.

That's FaceChan. It's not a clever name. It's simply exactly what it says.

---

## Why it became something more

For a long time this was just going to be a GitHub project. Something to show I could build it. A toy. A curiosity. I was just going to upload a part project that allowed for threads and replies with some 4chan methodology.

Then governments started getting involved.

Not in some distant theoretical way — in the specific, practical, accelerating way where legislation is being written by people who have clearly never used the internet as a tool for anything other than sending emails and reading headlines. The Online Safety Act. The DSA. The steady drumbeat of "think of the children" used to justify surveillance infrastructure that has nothing to do with children and everything to do with control.

Here's the thing. I *am* thinking of the children. I have one. And what I want for my child is not a sanitised, monitored, pre-approved internet where every word is logged against an identity and every opinion is subject to removal by whoever currently holds power. I want them to grow up in a world where people can speak freely — not without consequence, not without responsibility, but without the state holding the keys to the conversation.

That's the moment this stopped being a toy project and I decided I should complete it through to a point where others could install and use a complete application. Adding features like federation was a natural extension. 

I believe freedom of speech is in genuine danger. Not the pantomime version where people argue about whether offensive jokes should be allowed on private platforms. The real version — where the infrastructure of censorship is being quietly built into law, and once it's there it doesn't go away, and whoever comes next gets to use it however they like. We are building the cage now and assuming forever that only the good guys will hold the keys. That assumption has never been safe and it isn't safe now.

FaceChan is my response to that. A small one. Maybe an irrelevant one. But it's what I can do.

---

## What this is — and isn't

This is not a platform for hatred. I have a child. I've spent enough time on the internet to know exactly what the worst of it looks like. The compliance architecture in this software is real — it isn't decoration. The CSAM scaffolding, the moderation tiers, the operator responsibility framework — those exist because I believe that anonymity and safety are not opposites. They are harder to hold together than either alone, but the difficulty doesn't make it impossible or not worth attempting.

This is not a place designed for the worst of /b. It's designed for the possibility of the best of it.

What I hope FaceChan becomes — on whatever instances people choose to run — is somewhere people talk. Freely. About cooking and technology and politics and animals and the things that make them laugh. Without a platform deciding which opinions are acceptable this quarter. Without an algorithm deciding what they're allowed to care about. Without an advertiser's sensibilities sitting between a person and the thing they actually want to say.

I want people to come together in the oldest sense — not because an app told them they had something in common, but because they found each other and decided to talk.

---

## The gift

FaceChan is MIT licensed. It costs nothing. It will always cost nothing.

Kino is anonymous. That's not evasion — it's the point. The software doesn't need an author with a verified identity and a public profile. It needs to work. Fork it. Run it. Make it yours. Make it something I wouldn't recognise.

If it helps one person say something true that they couldn't say with their name attached — good.

If it helps one community form that couldn't have formed on a platform built to sell them things — good.

If it survives long enough that the next generation inherits something a little more open than what we were handed — then it was worth every hour.

That's all. Go build something.

— *Kino*
